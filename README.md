## Introduction
The human gut houses one of the most diverse microbiomes , .  Often, these microbial taxa are sorted into two distinct enterotypes or biomarkers, known broadly as *Bacteroides* and *Prevotella*, that carry many different characteristics from each other (Gorvitovskaia et al., 2016).  Metagenomics has typically been at the heart of many studies to better understand the gut microbiota, in order to learn how the presence and abundance of different taxa in this community help to support all sorts of essential functions for digestion and protection of the human gut and intestinal tract (Wang et al., 2015).  While the , there thankfully exist many different methods that can be applied to conduct taxonomic classification of gut microbiota, examine their abundances in the metagenome, and quantify different aspects of their overall diversity. 

As hinted above, one major factor whose effects have widely been investigated in human gut metagenomics is dietary habit, such as vegan, vegetarian, or omnivore (De Filippis et al., 2019).  A study conducted in 2019 by De Filippis et al., which investigated human gut microbomes of Italians using shotgun metagenomics, found that differing diets can actually affect the presence of certain microbiota, especially for one prevalent species, *Prevotella copri*.  They found that this species revealed different strains at different locations based on the types of foods consumed often, such as fiber-rich or omnivorous, revealing how diet can affect diversity at even the finest levels of taxonomy (De Filippis et al., 2019).  Interestingly enough, it also appeared to suggest how diets between different types of global populations, and in turn, the composition of gut microbiota, can further affect the ability of biological processes to consume certain types of food, most notably in non-Western countries like Italy (De Filippis et al., 2019).  Today, this species is now referred more commonly as *Segatella copri*, in line with the reclassification of many other species of this genus, from *Prevotella* to *Segatella* (Blanco-Miguez et al., 2023, Wang et al., 2026).

Studies such as these can prove most useful for explaining the essence of these connections between microbial taxonomy and lifestyle, in human health/disease as a whole and across populations (De Filippis et al., 2019).  However, it is also important that the composition and abundance of other taxa within the diversity should be investigated more thoroughly, especially at the species level, to understand exactly how they collectively support the necessary biological functions for food consumption and metabolism.  Hence, this taxonomic analysis seeks to expand on the significance of these connections, by applying different classification and diversity pipelines to compare how the abundances and diversity of certain microbiotic taxa in the human gut metagenomes differ across individuals with varying dietary patterns.  It will also attempt to explain how the presence and/or dominance of certain species across diets support digestive and metabolic functions and processes most associated with them.


## Methods
Raw short-read data for human gut microbiomes was randomly selected from samples used in a shotgun metagenomics study, with Illumina sequencing, from 74 Italians based on dietary habits, found on the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database (University of Naples Federico II, 2017).  A total of six sets of paired-end samples were obtained, with three sets belonging to individuals with vegan diets and the other three belonging to individuals with omnivorous diets (University of Naples Federico II, 2017).  Quality control was conducted on each individual paired-end sample using FastQC (v0.12.1) on 12 threads per sample, to check for sources of contamination, which were then compiled into overall reports with MultiQC (v1.33) (Andrews, 2010, Ewels et al., 2016).  Adapter content was then trimmed off each paired-end sample using *fastp* on default parameters, before repeating quality control reports a second time and compressing them (.gz files) (Chen et al., 2018).  Taxonomic classification for each set of paired-end samples (.gz) was done using k-mer classification in Kraken2 (v2.1.6), using a confidence level of 0.15 to reduce the likelihood of false positives (Wood et al., 2019).  These computations were run remotely in the Narval Compute Canada cluster at Digital Research Alliance of Canada (DRAC) on 128G memory and 16 cores per task, with the most recent Kraken2 Standard database as of February 26, 2026 (Langmead, 2026).  Finally, taxonomic abundances for each sample were re-estimated at species level from classification genus reads, using Bayesian Re-estimation of Abundance with KrakEN (Bracken v3.0.1) at 8 threads per sample (Lu et al., 2017).  These Bracken species reports were subsequently compiled and converted into a BIOM file using *kraken-biom* (v1.2.0) for further taxonomic analysis (Dabdoub, 2016).

Continuing in R (v4.5.2), the BIOM report of all abundance tables from Bracken was imported into the working environment with the *phyloseq* package (v1.54.2) and the *biomformat* package (v1.38.3) (McMurdie & Holmes, 2013, McMurdie & Paulson, 2026).  Relative abundance of the top 20 most abundant taxa across all dietary groups and samples were compared and visualized with barplots using *phyloseq* (v1.54.2) and the *microbiome* package (v1.32.0), to explore the diversity of classifications and determine the most prominent microbial species in each set of metagenomes (McMurdie & Holmes, 2013, Lahti & Shetty, 2017).  Alpha and beta diversity measures were also both calculated using functions from the *phyloseq* package.  For alpha diversity, three different indices were calculated for each sample and visualized using dot plots.  Additionally, the mean &plusmn; standard Deviation was calculated overall for each of these indices in the omnivore and vegan diet groups.  The Shannon index was calculated to understand and visualize the ovarall taxa evenness in a sample, while the Simpson index was calculated to determine taxa dominance between groups and/or samples.  The Chao1 index was also calculated to evaluate the amount of taxa richness per sample.  Meanwhile, beta diversity was compared between vegan and omnivore diet groups using Jaccard and Bray-Curtis distance measures for dissimilarities in microbiomes.  Both measures of beta diversity were then visualized with Principle Coordinate Analysis (PCoA) plots.  Additionally, a Permutational Multivariate Analysis of Variance Based on Distance (PERMANOVA) statistical test was run (using the default 999 permutations) to test for significance in differences between the two groups using the *vegan* package (v2.7-3) (Anderson, 2008, Oksanen et al., 2026).  Lastly, to determine the most significant differences in abundant taxa by diet, Analysis of Compositions of Microbiomes with Bias Correction 2 (ANCOM-BC2, v2.12.1) was used to conduct differential abundance analysis between the two groups, treating omnivore samples as the intercept (Lin & Peddada, 2020).  This analysis used the 5th percentile (0.05) of standard errors to define the *s0* constant, along with an adjusted p-value (*q*) cutoff of 0.05, and the default Holm-Bonferroni correction for multiple testing to reduce the false positive rate without being too fully conserative (Vinogradova et al., 2016).  Since adjusted p-values (*q*) returned a value of 1.00 for all samples after correction, though, the raw p-values (p) of taxa were used to subset the top 20 differentially abundant taxa to be visualized by effect size plot.

## Results
### Relative Taxonomic Abundance
Relative abundance for every sample was measured at the species level, with the top 20 taxa being ranked by highest abundance (Figure 1). In omnivore diets, the relative abundance between species looked more distributed for all the top 20 taxa.  Two species, though, *Bifidobacterium adolescentis* and *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, seemed to show higher relative abundance in their microbiomes.  In vegan diets, however, the abundance pattern seemed to be more distinct, as *Segatella copri*, the same species studied by De Filippis et al., showed a higher and more pronounced relative abundance across at least two of their samples (2019).

### Alpha Diversity
Alpha diversity was measured to quantify diversity within samples by examining the evenness, dominance, and richness of taxonomic species in the metagenomes.  Results were also compared between groups to evaluate how these measures differed in Italians based on omnivore and vegan diets.

#### Taxonomic Species Evenness - Shannon Index
The Shannon index was calculated to measure the amount of evenness in the overall distribution of all taxa for each sample (Figure 2).  When looking at the results, the mean &plusmn; SD evenness for omnivore diets was reported at about 3.67 &plusmn; 0.32, which was somewhat higher than the evenness for vegan diets, which was about 3.22 &plusmn; 0.18.  A closer look at Figure 2 showed that all three metagenomic samples belonging to the omnnivore diet expressed higher species evenness overall than the samples belonging to the vegan diet.  In the omnivorous group, the Shannon index ranged from about 3.45 to 4.04, a difference of 0.59.  This was due to one sample having a somewhat higher Shannon index as opposed to the other two samples, possibly highlighing a slightly skewed or inflated mean evenness.  In the vegan group, the Shannon Index ranged from 3.04 to 3.40, a difference of 0.36.  However, there was less difference observed between its three samples.

#### Taxonomic Species Dominance - Simpson Index
Next, the Simpson index was used to assess dominance of certain taxa in abundance per sample, as well as to quantify the extent to which they did over taxa with average or lower abundance (Figure 3).  Due to this index being calculated using the *phyloseq* program, it represented the effect of dominance exhibited by one or more taxa.  Looking at all samples in both groups, the Simpson indices were all notably closer to 1 than to 0.  Additionally, they ranged only from about 0.83 to 0.97, a difference of 0.14, suggesting a higher overall dominant effect of one or more specific taxa, regardless of diet type.  Omnivorous diets had a mean &plusmn; SD index of 0.94 &plusmn; 0.03, while for vegan diets, it was about 0.89 &plusmn; 0.06, a smaller difference between the groups.  In Figure 3, there appeared to be less spread in the samples for the omnivore group, while the vegan group seemed to show slightly more consistent spread in its samples.  The Simpson indices for the omnivore group ranged from approximately 0.95 to 0.97, a very minimal difference of 0.02.  On the other hand, the vegan group had a range of about 0.83 to 0.94, a slightly greater difference of 0.11.

#### Taxonomic Species Richness - Chao1 Index
Finally, the Chao1 index helped to estimate and better establish overall species richness, which included both observed taxa and potentially unobserved taxa within each metagenomic sample (Figure 4).  Interestingly, unlike the first two indices, however, the vegan group actually had a higher mean and lower SD of 463 &plusmn; 68, as opposed to the omnivore group, which had a lower mean and higher SD at 441 &plusmn; 109.  Figure 4 also showed that, for the omnivorous diets, the sample which had a higher Shannon index than the other two samples similarly also had a higher Chao1 index than them, and the spread between it and the other samples was visually a lot greater this time.  Once again, this might have pointed towards a skewed effect on mean richness.  The range for this group was from about 341 to 557, a major difference of 216.  On the other hand, the vegan group also had one sample that was different from the other two, having a lower Chao1 index than them.  However, this group's range of indices was from 386 to 514, a lower difference of 128, possibly suggesting less spread or skewedness.

### Beta Diversity
Beta diversty was calculated to determine and quantify the amount of variation between vegan and omnivore groups, as well as to investigate if and how much variation could be explained by dietary habit.  Two different types of distance measures for similarity were compared, these being the Jaccard (Figure 5) and Bray-Curtis distance measures (Figure 6).  However, when inspecting each of Figures 5 and 6, the sample data points did not appear to form recognizable clusters that could be distinguished by diet, instead appearing more randomly dispersed.  When using Jaccard distance, the largest source of variation, PCoA1, was able to explain 28.4% of the variation between groups, whereas the second largest source of variation, PCoA2, explained 26.5%.  This meant that the total amount of variation explained using Jaccard distance was about 54.9%.  For Bray-Curtis distance, the primary axes were able to explain slightly more variation by comparison.  PCoA1 explained 32.6% of variation, and PCoA2 explained 30.7%, leading to a higher explanation of 63.3%.

Then, when running a PERMANOVA for each type of distance measure, the statistical test revealed more evidence that there was no difference between the omnivore and vegan gut microbial communities.  When looking at each model, the R<sup>2</sup> value for Jaccard distance of about 0.21, suggesting that about 21% of its model should have been explainable by dietary habit.  Similarly, the R<sup>2</sup> value for Bray-Curtis distance also confirmed a value of about 0.21.  Even then, however, a closer look at the p-value of revealed that this R<sup>2</sup> value could not be treated as signficant, as both methods returned a non-significant p-value each of 0.5.

### Differential Abundance - ANCOM-BC2
The final part of this investigation was a differential abundance analysis between the vegan and omnivore groups, to determine which taxa were significantly different between dietary habits (Figure 7).  Unfortunately, when looking at the adjusted p-values (*q*), all taxa returned a non-significant value of 1.00.  This meant that it was not possible to determine significance of any major taxa with confidence, greatly affecting the interpretation of these findings.  As such, interpretation was limited to understanding these log fold changes as connections, or trends, to dietary habits in existing literature.  To determine the top 20 taxa for analysis, the unadjusted p-value (*p*) was utilized instead, with a level of significance at 0.05, to suggest the most likely differentially abundant taxa that best represented each group from its samples.  Based on this decision, Figure 7 illustrated a notable, nearly even split in the number of taxa represented more greatly in each group by log fold change.  The taxon with the greatest positive log fold change was *Akkermansia muciniphila*, suggesting, but not with certainty, that it was more abundant in the vegan group.  Conversely, the greatest negative log fold change came from *Catenibacterium mitsuokai*, possibly suggesting its likely higher abundance in the omnivore group.

## Discussion
This taxonomic analysis of human gut metagenomics sought to provide a more insightful context of how the abundance and diversity of many different microbiota were connected to essential biological functions of vegan and omnivorous dietary habits.  However, considering that a low sample size was often a highly major limitation in the analyses, the scope of interpretation for these results, in turn, was often difficult to justify with full confidence, making them rather less reliably effective on their own.  Nonetheless, there were still some key findings that emerged which helped to better illustrate the relevance of these other abundant taxa to human health/disease.

Looking at relative abundance, each group had 1-2 distinct taxa that showed a higher relative abundance compared to other species, which appeared to be more evenly distributed.  For omnivorous samples, these were *Bifidobacterium adolescentis* and *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*.  The *Bifidobacterium* genus contains a wide variety of species whose abundances typically differ based on function and location (Yin et al., 2023).  *B. adolescentis*, in particular, is a species that contains carbohydrate-active enzymes, allowing it to break down carbohydrates and resistant starches that could be found more abundantly in omnivore diets (Yin et al., 2023, Jung et al., 2019).  For *F. prausnitzii*, although the exact reasons for its abundance in omnivorous diet are not as clear in the literature, this microbe has often been studied in research related to gut disorders, namely obesity (Maioli et al., 2021).  It is well known for producing butyrate, a key product in inhibiting the IL-6 pathway, exerting anti-inflammatory properties to help regulate gut health (Maioli et al., 2021).  Considering that obesity is more likely to stem from greater intakes of fats, sugars, and proteins, which are more characteristic in an omnivorous diet, this is one explanation could potentially justify its greater abundance in omnivorous samples over vegan samples (Maioli et al., 2021).  Meanwhile, the most abundant species for vegan samples was *Segatella copri*, or *Prevotella copri*, which matched the same microbe studied by De Filippis et al. (2019, Wang et al., 2026).  *P. copri* often has many strong connections to vegan diets, as they are mostly able to break down and utilize plant polysaccharides from fruits and vegetables, and thus, they thrive much more in high-fiber environments (Fehlner-Peach et al., 2019).  This effect is especially best observed in geographical areas where plant-based diets are more prevalent, such as in rural Africa (De Filippo et al., 2010).

Then, taking a further look into the alpha diversity of samples, it was determined that, on average, omnivorous samples appeared to have more species evenness in terms of abundance distribution, as well as strong dominance of one or more taxa, according to the Shannon and Simpson indices respectively.  However, it was the vegan samples that seemed to show more species richness on average when investigating the Chao1 index.  When comparing this to prior studies, this seems to somewhat reflect similar disagreement in the scientific literature for determining the dietary type with higher alpha diversity, with either these indices or others also having been similarly used.  For example, one study by Fackelmann et al., which involved over 21,000 individuals, found that species richness in omnivores, using the Chao1 index, was higher than in vegans or vegetarians, which opposes the findings of this analysis (2025).  In contrast, a study by Sun et al., which applied all three measures, along with Ace, for 16S rRNA sequences of microbiota in stool samples, found evidence for higher diversity in vegan diets than omnivores, especially with lowered intake of fats, meats, or carbohydrates (2023).  Even yet, a few studies, such as the randomized controlled trial by Kohnert et al., which used Chao1 and Simpson with many others, found no significant differences between diets during a four-week intervention (2024).  Overall, one of the main takeaways from these results suggests that the type of metric used to calculate diversity, along with the different species abundances based on dietary habits, are essential for determining the context to which we consider alpha diversity to be greater (Fackelmann et al., 2025, Sun et al., 2023, Kohnert et al., 2024).  Together, these underscore the need to evaluate alpha diversity with respect or consideration of other factors relevant to the focus of the comparison that is made.

For beta diversity, the PCoA analysis for Jaccard and Bray-Curtis distances showed that about 55-63% of the overall variation could be explained by the primary axes of the model, though the samples did not appear to form distinct clusters with their groups.  PERMANOVA also provided further evidence that supported the lack of distinction between groups with a low model R<sup>2</sup> value that was found to be non-significant, further suggesting that diet could not be used to explain the community differences of gut microbiota at the beta diversity level.  Looking at this analysis a bit deeper, one potentially important consideration to understand is how the gut microbiome can experience even further variation at the individual level (The Human Microbiome Project Consortium, 2012).  Other external factors, such as environment or genetics, can also contribute to further variations in the exact composition and abundance of specific microbes in the gut (The Human Microbiome Project Consortium, 2012).  This can further be cemented when looking at studies like the one conducted by Turnbaugh et al. in 2008, which looked at gut microbiomes in twins using murine models.  In essence, these results highlight and justify how important it is to consider further sources of variation that could potentially affect gut microbiota even further between samples per group, especially when measuring beta diversity.

Lastly, the differential abundance analysis revealed a mostly distinct separation in taxa abundance between the vegan and omnivorous groups.  Once again, though, they, along with all other taxa, were not proven statistically significant, so it was not possible to evaluate true significance of these differences.  All the same, though, it was still possible to understand how these findings were tied to existing trends or connections to the scientific literature on health and disease.  For vegan diets, the results suggested that *Akkermansia muciniphila* was the most enriched taxon for its group.  *A. muciniphila* is an essential bacterium that resides in the gastrointestinal gut, responsible for the degradation of a protective mucus layer known as mucin (Collado et al., 2007).  It has been found to inhabit and colonize this area of the digestive system as early as infant stages, and its role in mucin degradation actually helps to release nutrients from the layer that are essential for the growth of other necessary microbiota, especially in this environment (Collado et al., 2007).  Mucin itself serves as a primary protective barrier against pathogens responsible for most digestive diseases, with many of its *O*-glycans contributing to this role through the production of *n*-butyrates to support immune cell growth, which could be potentially further elevated with a vegan diet (Yamada et al., 2019, Satchithanandam et al., 1996).  In most murine models, studies have found that dietary fiber has often played an important role in increasing the production of mucin in the gastrointestinal tract (Satchithanandam et al., 1996).  Together, *A. muciniphila* and mucin help to sustain a regulatory function supporting both immune protection and microbial growth (Collado et al., 2007).  On the other hand, for omnivorous diets, the results suggested *Catenibacterium mitsuokai* as the most abundant in comparison.  Usually extracted from human stool samples, *C. mitsuokai* is considered a Gram-positive bacterium that specifically handles anaerobic functions in the digestive tract (Kageyama & Benno, 2000).  It is worth noting that, in contrast, to this analysis, *C. mitsuokai* has also often been the subject of disease research in the human gut, though for its connections to the pathogenicity of hepatocellular carcinogenesis, rather than maintaning gut health (Zhang et al., 2025).  However, as more animal studies have revealed, *C. mitsuokai* has also been quite influential in the gut microbial community, supporting a number of anaerobic metabolic functions often observed in an omnivorous diet, such as fats and lipids (Wei et al., 2025).  In addition, as more fats, along with proteins and sugars, get introduced with an omnivorous diet, often seen in Western diets, more shifts in the microbiota occur, which could potentially justify the presence and role of *C. mitsuokai* in this group (Turnbaugh et al., 2009).  While this analysis unfortunately cannot confirm the significance of these results, it has shown that they still reveal both interesting and potentially useful details and connections about their roles and essence in affecting or maintaining digestive functions and human gut health.
